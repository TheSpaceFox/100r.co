engine care

We have learned a lot about diesel engines over the years, we combined these learnings into 3 categories:

Service inspection checklist. What parts need to be serviced on a diesel engine.
Service items. What to change/check daily, monthly and annually.
Spares list. What spares to carry. The amount of spares depends on the duration of the trip. A long-distance cruiser will require more spares.
General notes. A collection of notes on engine-related parts, like on oil, coolant, belts, coolant pump, salt water pump, mufflers, belts, zincs, ventilation etc.
Troubleshooting. Common problems, their causes, and how to solve them.

Service items

Annual

Change:

Change at schedule intervals. 100 hours, or once per year, whichever comes first.

  • Engine oil & filter (aim for fall, after summer season)
  • Transmission oil (aim for fall, after summer season)
  • Fuel filters (aim for fall, after summer season)
  • Cold start

Checks:

Do checks in the spring before start of cruising season.

  • Impeller in salt water pump | Damage and wear
  • Sea strainer | Clean
  • Antisiphon valve | Operation and condition
  • Zinc/anodes | Condition
  • Exchaust mixing elbow | Condition
  • Muffler/hose | Condition
  • Hoses | Condition and chafing
  • Belts | Wear and damage
  • Coolant | PH & freeze protection
  • Air filter | Dirty and damage
  • Stuffing box | Leakage and integrity
  • Mounts | Condition and tightness
  • Wiring & cables | Chafing
  • Battery | Fluid levels and connections
  • Alternator | Charging and regulation

Monthly

  • Battery fluids and fluid levels
  • Stuffing box adjustment

Daily

  • Fluid levels | Oil and coolant
  • Primary fuel filter | Visually check
  • Engine | Visually check
  • Sea strainer | Visually check

Oil notes: It is best to change the oil in the winter, not in the spring so that the engine can sit in clean oil. After using the engine, the oil takes on a black color, a byproduct of combustion. Modern types of oil prevent sediment formation in the oil pan, so there is no need to clean it. When changing the oil, always change the oil filter (insert trick not to make a mess). When adding a new filter, coat the o-ring with oil and tighten by hand (a 3/4 turn is enough). Check the engine after replacing the filter to see if there is a leak. Overtightening the filter can make it very difficult to remove.

There are many kinds of oil, each with their own viscosity (SAE class, flow properties as a function of the operating temperature) and API (American petroleum institute) rating. Diesel engine formulated oil will have additives not required in gasoline engines. Diesel engines need a C rating (CC being the minimum). Older engines don't need high performance oil (CF is the oldest type). If a bottle of oil has the text 'energy conserving' it means that its viscosity allows for reduced fuel consumption (less fuel burnt to turn components inside engine), it also reduces friction. Using synthetic oils is fine, because it is less viscous it is fantastic in cold climates. Synthetics allow allow for longer service intervals. As a general rule, don't mix oils of different types (viscosities). In the Pacific Northwest, 15W40 is the norm. Engine oils of class 15W40 are multigrade oils.

Coolant notes: Important to test the antifreeze once in a while to check the level of glycol to prevent freezing and to check the PH of the coolant. As it ages, coolant turns too acidic and can corrode the unit. It is possible to buy pre-mixed coolant (more volume) or a concentrate (less volume, but you need to add 50% water). There is long life coolant, and regular coolant, never mix the two kinds. They come in a variety of colors, differing from diesel, or oil so that it is easy to identify a leak.

The heat exchanger water pump is metal and integral to the unit, it cannot be serviced. If for whatever reason it fails, it must be replaced entirely. The coolant water pump has a drain hole, if the seal of the pump breaks it may leak. Lifting paint or discoloration near the joint is indicative of such a leak.

Belts notes: Belts stretch overtime, and must be checked. An alternator is a load on a belt, if it is too tight it becomes a strain on both the alternator and the water pump. An engine that is overheating can damage a belt. If the belt starts to create dust on your engine it is because it is too tight, too thin or because the alternator is too big (too demanding for the engine size).

Salt water pump: Service once per year. Take out the impeller to look at it. If the engine runs dry for too long (no water, due to closed thru-hull or blockage), it can damage the impeller. Removing the impeller with an impeller puller is ideal (especially for large impellers), but otherwise it's possible to remove using two screwdrivers as leverage, if using this method be careful not to damage the water pump housing. When checking an impeller, check that the nubs are nice and round, and that all the arms are straight. When inside the housing, the arms have a curve but should spring back when the impeller is taken out. It is important for the arms to be straight so that they follow the inside walls of the housing perfectly, if they do not touch the walls there will be no vacuum and the pump won't pull water through. If an engine is allowed to sit for too long, the arms can set in the wrong position, if that happens the impeller should be replaced.
Check the impeller of cracks between the arms, a severely damaged impeller will have arms missing. If it is missing arms, it will be necessary to check the engine for the lost bits (they can clog the system). The arms usually find their way to the heat exchanger. The impeller doesn't need to go back in any specific way, adding dishsoap to help slide it back in helps. Dish soap is also useful if the impeller is hard to remove. If replacing the impeller, replace the gasket too (spare impellers usually come with it). If there is no spare gasket, it is possible to make out of thin paper, or to use liquid gasket. The pump will have drain holes in case the seal fails.

Heat exchanger pump notes: Salt water runs through the heat exchanger to cool the coolant. There may be accumulations of salt at the mouth (or forgotten impeller bits), which ought to be cleared. If flow is restricted, the motor will be running hot. It is a good ideal to periodically change the seal, the seal helps keep the coolant and salt water separate. If the coil inside of the heat exchange is sludgy, it is because the coolant has stayed in too long.

Muffler: A muffler quiets the exhaust, and acts as a water lock. It is at its fullest when the engine is not running. Mufflers have drains, they can corrode and must be checked periodically. A muffler can be made of plastic, or metal.

Hoses: The hose from the engine to the muffler ought to be as straight as possible, and at a slant (high from engine to low at muffler) to prevent the pooling of water. If the engine has been overheating, check this hose for damage. Hoses used with engines have many layers, and when there is excessive heat theses layers can begin to part and retain water. For engine use hard wire hoses are best. Tie hoses down to avoid chafe. Check ends of hoses often, they fail often.
Use quality clamps, like AWAB or T-bolt clamps, designs that are smooth, that don't have sharp inner bits that can cut into a hose. When applying a clamp over a hose to secure it to a part of the engine, make note where the hose intake/outake ends so that you don't run the risk of compressing the hose itself, restricting flow and damaging the hose. If there is no room to put two clamps (the nipple isn't long enough), put one.

Zinc: Check zincs often, replace before completely worn. Heat exchange uses pencil zincs. They have a standard length, but not all heat exchange zinc housings can accomodate it. Before placing it, insert a pencil in the hole to see how deep the hole goes, and trim the zinc if necessary. Not cutting the zinc can result in breakage, and leaks. When screwing in new pencil zinc, add liquid seal at the head of the pencil zinc to prevent leakages.

Ventilation: 60°C (140°F) is the max heat an engine room ought to have. Ventilation is more important for bigger engines. Small engines may have passive ventilation, with the air intake sucking up air from the engine room itself and with the outtake coming out of an aft dorade. The forward motion of a boat is usually enough to move the heat, but otherwise a small fan fitted to the outtake duct is useful. Air ducts are sized to the engine power, make sure nothing is crushing the duct. Check periodically for delamination of the duct.

General tips: If white salt crystals form round seals, it means that the joints are leaking and the gasket must be replaced. Cast iron corrodes from the inside out, outside signs include brown, rusty spots. Keep an absorbent sheet under engine, so that leaks (should there be any) can be easily identified.

Troubleshooting:

Problem: Exhaust leakage into boat.
Symptom: Water and or fumes in boat.

  • Cause 1: corroded exhaust elbow.
    Solution: replace exhaust elbow.
  • Cause 2: corroded muffler.
    Solution: replace muffler.
  • Cause 3: hose failure.
    Solution: replace hose.

Problem: low engine power.
Symptom: cannot attain full revs, black smoke.

  • Cause 1: plugged exhaust elbow.
    Solution: replace exhaust elbow.
  • Cause 2: collapsed exhaust hose.
    Solution: replace hose.
  • Cause 3: clogged air filter.
    Solution: replace/clean filter element (some can be cleaned).
  • Cause 4: insufficient air supply.
    Solution: ensure ventilation is adequate.
  • Cause 5: rope around prop.
    Solution: remove rope.

Problem: engine flooded with water.

Symptom: engine will not turn over.

  • Cause 1: antisiphon valve has failed closed.
    Solution: flush water from engine drain exhaust system and run-up. Service antisiphon valve.
  • Cause 2: exhaust elbow has failed.
    Solution: flush water from engine drain exhaust system and replace exhaust elbow and run-up.
  • Cause 3: excessive cranking without starting.
    Solution:flush water from engine, drain exhaust system and run-up. (close thru-hull if cranking too much to avoid re-flooding the engine, remember to re-open it when it starts).

Problem: exhaust water temperature is high.
Symptom: steam fro exhaust water, possible elevated engine temperature.

  • Cause: exhaust elbow restricted at water injection point.
    Solution: replace or repair exhaust elbow.

Problem: running hot.
Symptom 1: temperature gauge reads high up to 93°C (200°F).
Symptom 2: steam in exhaust especially under higher loads.

  • Cause 1: debris clogging intake strainer.
    Solution: clear debris.
  • Cause 2: damaged impeller.
    Solution: replace impeller.
  • Cause 3: restricted heat exchanger.
    Solution: clean.
  • Cause 4: restricted exhaust elbow.
    Solution: clean or remove elbow.
  • Cause 5: insufficient engine room ventilation.
    Solution: reinstate adequate ventilation.
  • Cause 6: loose fresh water pump belt.
    Solution: re-tension belt.
  • Cause 7: faulty thermostat.
    Solution: replace thermostat.

Problem: overheating.
Symptom 1: temperature gauge reads very high over 93°C (200°F).
Symptom 2: alarm sounds.

  • Cause: loss of coolant.
    Solution: locate/repair cause of loss.
  • Cause: broken fresh water pump belt.
    Solution: replace belt.
  • Cause: failed fresh water pump.
    Solution: replace pump.
  • Cause: leaking head gasket.
    Solution: replace gasket, or consult mechanic.

Problem: running cool.
Symptom: temperature gauge reads low.

  • Cause 1: faulty thermostat.
    Solution: replace thermostat (possible to remove it temporarily so alarm stops).
  • Cause 2: stuck open.
    Solution: replace thermostat.
